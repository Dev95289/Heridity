Heredity

Heredity is how parents pass traits to their children through genes. Genes are tiny parts of DNA, which is like an instruction book for building things like eye color, height, or even the shape of your nose. For example, if your mom and dad both have brown eyes, you might get brown eyes too because of the genes they give you. But things like the sun or what you eat can also change how some traits show up.

Learn more from Heredity Definition. It says heredity also helps species change over many years through something called natural selection.

Mendel’s Laws of Inheritance

Gregor Mendel was a scientist who studied pea plants a long time ago (between 1856 and 1863). He discovered two big rules, or laws, about how traits are passed from parents to kids. These rules help us understand genetics, which is the study of genes.

  • Law of Segregation: Every parent has two copies of each gene—one from their mom and one from their dad. When they have a child, they give just one copy. So, if a parent has a gene for brown eyes and one for blue eyes, the child gets one or the other, not both.
    • Example: Mendel crossed a purple-flowered pea plant (PP) with a white-flowered one (pp). The first kids all had purple flowers (Pp) because purple is stronger. But when those plants made new seeds, some were white (pp). This shows genes split up, or segregate. See more at Mendelian Inheritance.
  • Law of Independent Assortment: Different traits don’t stick together. For example, the color of a pea flower doesn’t decide its seed shape. This mixes things up and makes every child a little different.
    • Example: A plant with purple flowers and round seeds can have kids with purple flowers and wrinkled seeds. This is explained in Mendel’s Laws.

These laws are super important, but sometimes there are tricky cases that don’t follow them exactly.

How is Sex Determined?

In people, whether you’re a boy or a girl depends on special genes called chromosomes. Girls have two X chromosomes (XX), and boys have one X and one Y (XY). The mom always gives an X, but the dad can give an X or a Y. If he gives a Y, you get a boy (XY); if he gives an X, you get a girl (XX).

For example, if a baby is a boy, it’s because the dad’s Y chromosome mixed with the mom’s X. Sometimes unusual things like XXY happen, but that’s rare. Check out Sex Determination in Humans for more.

Evolution & Classification

Evolution is how animals and plants change slowly over a long time. It happens through natural selection, where the ones best suited to their home survive and have babies. Classification is putting living things into groups based on how they’re related, like family members.

For example, birds grew feathers to fly better—that’s evolution. And we group humans, chimpanzees, and gorillas together because they come from the same big family long ago. Scientists use DNA now to figure this out, though they sometimes argue about the best way. See Evolution and Classification.

Acquired and Inherited Traits

Traits are things about you. Some you get while you live (acquired), and some you’re born with (inherited).

  • Acquired Traits: These happen during your life and don’t pass to your kids. Like if you learn to play piano or get a tan from the sun—it’s yours, not your baby’s.
  • Inherited Traits: These come from your parents’ genes and can go to your kids. For example, if your mom has blue eyes, you might too.

A tan won’t make your child’s skin darker, but eye color can pass on. Learn more at Acquired and Inherited Traits.

Homologous and Analogous Organs

Organs in different animals can tell us about evolution.

  • Homologous Organs: These look alike inside but do different jobs. Like a human arm and a bat wing—they have the same bones but one grabs, the other flies. This means they had the same ancestor long ago.
  • Analogous Organs: These do the same job but look different inside. Like a bird wing and an insect wing—both fly, but one’s feathers and the other’s not. They grew alike because of where they live.

What are Fossils?

Fossils are old remains or marks of animals and plants from millions of years ago, like bones or footprints turned into rock. They show us what lived before and how things changed.

For example, dinosaur bones tell us big reptiles were here once. Fossils help us see evolution and old environments. Read more at Fossils.

Human Evolution

Human evolution is how we went from early ape-like creatures to modern people (Homo sapiens) over millions of years. Here are some big steps:

  • Australopithecus afarensis: Lived about 3.7 million years ago, walked on two legs—like “Lucy.”
  • Homo habilis: Around 2.4 million years ago, started using tools.
  • Homo erectus: About 1.8 million years ago, moved out of Africa with bigger brains.
  • Neanderthals: Lived until about 40,000 years ago, close cousins to us.
  • Homo sapiens: Us! Started about 300,000 years ago in Africa, now everywhere.

Timelines can change with new finds. See Human Evolution.
